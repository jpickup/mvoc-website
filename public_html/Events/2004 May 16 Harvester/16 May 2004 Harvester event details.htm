THE HARVESTER RELAY
15 -16 MAY 2004

 

EVENT DETAILS

Access
Pippingford Park is a turning off the main A22 London-Eastbourne trunk road. Easy access from the motorway network, either M25 junction 6 or M23 junction 10. East Grinstead railway station is about 6 miles away. Teams from the more distant parts of the country should note that Gatwick Airport is only 15 miles away. The taxi firm on the Gatwick Airport web site (unlikely to be the cheapest) quoted £32 for a pre-booked car capable of taking four people plus baggage from the airport to Pippingford.
 
Terrain
Pippingford Park is a private estate formed by enclosing part of Ashdown Forest, which is part of the High Weald Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty. The area is bisected by a string of lakes along Steel Forge Stream, which is famous for Pooh Sticks Bridge (unfortunately outside Pippingford) and is a tributary of the River Medway. Most of the park slopes down to the stream; the height difference between the highest and lowest parts of the map is over 100m.

Pippingford is now an army training area, although privately owned. Live munitions are not used, so there should be no risk from "suspicious objects".

The park is also used intensively by fallow deer. These spend the night on Ashdown Forest proper, and migrate into Pippingford for the day, preferring the company of the army to that of the general public and their dogs.

Three sorts of terrain predominate. The area around the start & finish is abandoned grazing that is now kept mown by the deer. Very fast, but not very technical.

There is a lot of semi-natural woodland, mostly deciduous. Oak is commonest, but most trees native to southern England can be found somewhere. Most of the woodland is very runnable; the deer help by trimming the lower foliage, and there is little green on the map. The bluebells should be in full bloom at the time of the event.

The third terrain type is open heath, with heather and long grass. Running through this is moderately hard work, but Pippingford is too well drained for the extensive areas of deep marsh and tussock grass for which Ashdown Forest is notorious. Again, the deer help by creating lots of little (unmapped) paths.

The area is almost completely free of brambles and gorse, and the army don't seem to have left any wire lying around. Two millennia of use for iron working and military activities have left a wealth of earth features to add to the natural re-entrants for control sites.

 Lap Lengths and Course Patterns
Please note that the Lap lengths are provisional and subject to test running of the courses. The actual lengths will be adjusted following test running with the aim of hitting the winning-time targets.
 
 A course: 7 laps, planned winning time 7 hours
 B course: 5 laps, planned first all-ladies team time 5 hours

 Both courses are being planned to be 2/3rds (by time) in the dark, 1/3rd in daylight
 
 A course 1st lap start: 00:30
 B course 1st lap start: 01:45

 Sunrise is due to be at 05:07.
 
 The A course lap pattern will be:
 1st lap 7km
 2nd lap 5km
 3rd lap 9km
 4th lap 7km
 5th lap 9km
 6th lap 5km
 7th lap 12km
 
 The B course lap pattern will be:
 1st lap 5km
 2nd lap 7km
 3rd lap 4km TD3 (Red standard with reflective controls)
 4th lap 5km
 5th lap 7km
 
The lengths quoted are to illustrate the ratio of lap lengths.
 
The winning course A team should expect it to get light during lap 5 whilst the winning course B team should expect dawn during lap 4.

Prizegiving
